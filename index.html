How to Get Good at Anything

Introduction

  • boils down to thinking critically

  • what does “good” even mean?

this isn’t just navel-gazing, or dodging the question: i genuinely think most people stop at the absolute minimum for good and go no further… if you think about it for even a second, you end up with something pretty useful, and then you can move on to the next steps

Take something simple, like board games. Many people would define being good at board games as, “you win more than most people, and you can beat people who are also good.” But, the thing is that “winning at the game” is ipso facto being good at the game, this definition is tautological. Furthermore, it isn’t remotely prescriptive: if you tell a neophyte board gamer to “try to win,” they’ll swiftly drop from your tutelage. So, again, what does being good look like? For any given game, it probably means being able to come up with a strategy that’s robust to opponents trying to do the same, and some degree of flexibility to switch strategies, and a little bit of luck (in the form of being poised to take advantage of opportunities). So, it’s about analysis. What do the mechanics of the game encourage you to do as the player? How can you minimize bad outcomes when things don’t go your way, and how can you maximize your advantage when they do?

While this Board Game Winner’s Framework doesn’t do much to teach you how to be a master chess player, for instance, it does teach you how, if you will, be good at being good at chess. Your questions to other chess players stop being, “how do I win?” and start being, “how do I make XXX opening less risky?” You start looking at your winning games as being more or less good than others (did you scrape out a victory? was it a crushing defeat? how did your strategy solidify over the course of the game?). Ditto with your losses: a narrow loss is still a drop in Elo, but you know that you’ve improved when your friend who used to clown on you starts to break a sweat in order to get checkmate.

There’s an increased granularity that comes from coming up with even an okay answer to, “what does good mean?” You ask better questions, you feel better about yourself, you’re more able to evaluate your progress.
