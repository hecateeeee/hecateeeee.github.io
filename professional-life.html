You Need a Profession

hex

A hobby is done for the love of the game. Presumably, this makes it more authentic an experience. At the very least it rejects the neoliberal idea of treating oneself as an “asset” to be “invested” in. But despite the bulwark erected in defense of hobbies qua hobbies, we’ve been tricked into something just as insidious: the idea of a profession as something merely monetizable. If for no other reason than that professions are paid (or, at least, compensated), this dupe is easier to pull off than convincing people to sell their cross stitches on Etsy. Now, we find ourselves hating our jobs and doing them only because they make us money, with the coercion of starvation and homelessness. There used to be; in some distant, hypothetical past; a pride in doing something well enough that you were known for it, that you could make money off of it. You paid the cobbler, or the barkeep, or the computer programmer, because they were passionate about and skilled at their craft. It was their profession, their trade.

The cobblers and the programmers weren’t spending their free time doing their trade. They put on their metaphorical or literal aprons,and they got their hands metaphorically or literally dirty. Then they went home and did their hobbies. I can think of a number of things I like being paid for that I don’t really do as hobbies. I’m a software engineer by trade, but unlike a (highly visible) subset of programmers, I don’t really program in my spare time. Other than the occasional toy problem that is patently best solved by a toy program, I don’t really program for the sake of programming. Don’t get me wrong: I love to program. I love thinking about architecture, I love debugging, I love talking to clients (including other programmers) to get design specs. I love perf testing and unit testing and CI and all the good things. I love small programs that do dinky text processing. I love it all. When I’m on the clock. Going even farther back, I used to work in the A/V department in the student union when I was an undergrad. I talked to clients, set up and struck events, ran cables, tested connectivity, operated a sound board, and so on. I coiled cables in the storage room and sorted things. These are all things I loved doing as a job, nearly none of which (from soundboard operating to coiling cables) I do as hobbies.

My hobbies are outdoors, like gardening and hiking. They’re hands on, like cross stitch and brazilian jiu-jitsu. They’re not things I want to get paid to do. I don’t think you could pay me enough to get me to sell my cross stitching labor. Maybe I would for, say, a million dollars a project, but that would be an offer I wouldn’t refuse. Regardless of what the fair theoretical price is for cross stitching skills, it’s not a million bucks a pop, so the hypothetical isn’t particularly useful.

There’s something about getting compensated to write code or coil cables that I need to want to do them. Correspondingly, there are things you couldn’t reasonably pay me to do. It’s not even a stand against late capitalism corrupting things, though that is personally compelling to me. I guess it’s more about early capitalism corrupting things. The eroding of the work/life barrier required to try to convince people to monetize their hobbies actually started with trying to encourage people to, uh, hobbetize their monies. Passionate people, to use a ruined word, do better work and don’t complain when you overwork them. If someone treats a job like a hobby, they’re eminently more exploitable.

We figured out some time in the recent past that, “do you like this hobby enough to do it as a profession?” is a bad question. Now, the question, “do you like this job enough to do it as a hobby?” is a side to a same coin. The conclusion to draw here is that neither is about how much you like some activity, but how you like that activity. There are, it seems, multiple ways to enjoy things. Some of these ways work well for hobbies, and some for careers. If you need hobbies, you also need a profession. It isn’t enough just to get paid for a thing. You have to like getting paid for it.
